What types of pipes do you stock?
We carry a comprehensive range of pipe types to suit every project: PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride for cold water supply), CPVC (Chlorinated PVC for hot water), uPVC (Unplasticised PVC for pressure lines and sewerage), HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene for underground, agricultural, and industrial use), and PPRC (Polypropylene Random Copolymer for hot and cold plumbing). Yes — the PVC and HDPE water supply pipes we stock are food-grade certified and safe for potable (drinking) water applications. Always look for the "WS" (Water Supply) marking on the pipe to confirm food-grade suitability. If you need pipes specifically for drinking water systems, let us know and we will guide you to the right product.

Technical
What is the difference between PVC and HDPE?
PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride) is a rigid pipe material best suited for above-ground water supply, drainage, and sewerage. It is easy to cut, join with solvent cement, and is cost-effective for most residential plumbing applications.

HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene) is a flexible, highly durable pipe ideal for underground installations, agricultural irrigation, industrial pipelines, and areas with ground movement. HDPE is joined by butt fusion or electrofusion welding and offers superior chemical resistance and a longer service life — typically 50+ years underground. Choose HDPE for underground and agricultural use; PVC for above-ground and drainage applications.
What pressure rating do I need?
Pressure ratings (PN — Nominal Pressure in bar) indicate the maximum continuous operating pressure a pipe can handle. As a general guide:

• PN6 — Low pressure, suitable for drainage and gravity-fed systems
• PN10 — Standard domestic water supply (most homes)
• PN16 — Higher pressure applications, water mains, pumped systems
• PN20+ — Industrial and commercial high-pressure systems

For typical household water supply in Pakistan, PN10 is usually sufficient. If you are unsure, share your project details with us and our team will advise the correct pressure rating.
How do I calculate pipe sizing for my project?
Pipe sizing depends on the flow rate required, pipe length, number of outlets, and acceptable pressure drop. For a typical home:

• Main supply line: 1" or 1¼" diameter
• Branch lines to bathrooms/kitchen: ¾" or 1"
• Individual fixture connections: ½"

For larger buildings or commercial projects, a hydraulic calculation based on peak demand flow rates is recommended. Our experienced staff can help you estimate the right sizes for standard residential projects — just describe your layout to us in store or via WhatsApp.
What fittings are compatible with my pipes?
Fittings must match both the pipe material and pipe size (outer diameter). Key compatibility rules:

• PVC/uPVC pipes: Use solvent cement PVC fittings of the same pressure class
• CPVC pipes: Use CPVC-specific fittings — do not mix with standard PVC
• HDPE pipes: Use HDPE compression fittings or welded HDPE fittings
• PPRC pipes: Use PPRC fittings heat-fused with a polyfusion welding tool

Always match the brand and pressure rating of fittings to your pipes where possible. Our staff will help you select the correct compatible fittings when you purchase pipes from us.
How do I join HDPE pipes?
HDPE pipes can be joined using several methods depending on pipe size and application:

1. Butt Fusion Welding — The most common method for larger diameter pipes. The pipe ends are heated on a flat plate and then pressed together to form a permanent leak-free joint. Requires a butt fusion machine.

2. Electrofusion Welding — An electrofusion fitting with embedded heating elements is placed over the pipe ends and electrically heated to fuse the joint. Ideal for tight spaces.

3. Compression Fittings — For smaller diameters (up to 63mm), mechanical compression fittings can be used without heat. Easy to install without special tools.

For most projects, we recommend butt fusion or compression fittings. Ask us for guidance on the right joining method for your specific application.
What solvent cement should I use for PVC pipes?
Use a PVC solvent cement (also called PVC adhesive or PVC glue) that is matched to your pipe type. For uPVC/PVC cold water pipes, use standard PVC solvent cement. For CPVC hot water pipes, use CPVC-rated solvent cement which is formulated to withstand higher temperatures. Always clean and prime the pipe surface before applying cement, ensure the joint is pushed firmly and held for 30 seconds, and allow adequate curing time before pressure testing (at least 24 hours). We stock a range of PVC cements and primers in our store.
